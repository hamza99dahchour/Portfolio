From general engineer to Data Scientist: my journey between Calais, Finland and Paris

CareerJourneyData

My path to data science wasn't linear from the start. It begins in a scientific preparatory class (PSI track), continues through a general engineering school, crosses an Erasmus exchange in Finland, and today takes shape through a Master's in Data Science & Business Intelligence on a work-study program at Carrefour. Here's what this journey has taught me.

Prep school: rigor above all

Two years of preparatory classes in the PSI track mainly taught me one thing: methodological rigor matters as much as the final answer. In data science, that reflex remains invaluable — a poorly framed model produces wrong results with the same confidence as a well-built one.

General engineering school: learning how to learn

My engineering degree in computer science, with a specialization in Data Science, at École d'ingénieurs du Littoral-Côte-d'Opale, gave me a broad technical foundation — development, systems, networks, databases — before specializing. That breadth of training is an underrated asset in data science: a data scientist who also understands infrastructure (Docker, Cloud, pipelines) communicates more easily with technical teams and ships projects that are actually deployable, not just notebooks.

Finland: stepping out of my comfort zone

A year on Erasmus exchange at Centria University of Applied Sciences, in Kokkola, was a shift in scale — personal as much as academic: a new working language, a new teaching culture, a new way of approaching projects independently. It's also during this period that I consolidated my professional English (B2 level), now a daily working tool.

The field: Pole Star, then Carrefour

My final-year internship at Pole Star SA was my first complete experience of an end-to-end data project — from data collection to the final product presented to teams. My current work-study program at Carrefour, as part of the EDC Master's, goes even further: it confronted me with a real business problem (forecasting demand for fresh products) using real data, operational constraints, and above all, non-technical end users to convince.

"A generalist background isn't a detour on the way to data science: it's often what makes it possible to ship projects that are genuinely useful."

What I take away from this journey

Each step — prep school, engineering school, Finland, Pole Star, then Carrefour — added a different building block: rigor, technical versatility, adaptability, field experience, and finally the ability to turn a statistical model into an operational decision. I believe it's this combination, more than any single isolated skill, that defines how I approach a data science project today.
