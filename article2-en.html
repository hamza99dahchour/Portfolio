The "barbecue effect": when weather and public holidays upend retail demand

Data ScienceSHAPRetail

Among the most striking findings of my thesis on forecasting fresh-product demand at Carrefour is what I called the "barbecue effect." It's a good example of what SHAP analysis can reveal: interactions between variables that a classic linear approach simply can't capture.

An effect that isn't a simple sum

Intuitively, one might think the effect of a public holiday on demand simply adds to the effect of warm weather. That's not what the data shows. When the two conditions combine — good weather and a public holiday — demand for certain fresh-product categories rises by 45% compared with a normal weekend, a figure higher than the sum of the two effects taken separately.

This is a sign of a genuine interaction: the public holiday amplifies the weather effect, and vice versa. A model that treated these two variables independently would systematically underestimate demand in this exact scenario — that is, precisely in the moments when a stock-out costs the most, for both the retailer and the customer.

Different temperature thresholds depending on the product

SHAP analysis also made it possible to identify temperature thresholds specific to each product category, rather than a generic weather effect. Demand for chilled drinks accelerates sharply from 22°C onward, while demand for prepared salads follows a different dynamic: it reverses around 18°C. These thresholds aren't arbitrary — they correspond to real tipping points in purchasing behavior, invisible in a simple moving average.

"A public holiday doesn't add to the weather effect: it multiplies it."

Why this matters for a store

This kind of result only matters if it becomes actionable. Translated into operational language, it gives a simple rule for a department manager: ahead of a public holiday forecast with warm weather, the answer isn't to slightly increase the order, but to anticipate a demand spike well above that of a "regular" public holiday or a simple sunny weekend.

This is exactly the kind of insight that explainability (SHAP) can surface from a Machine Learning model into a concrete business decision — and, in my view, it's what separates a model that "predicts well" from a model that's genuinely useful.
